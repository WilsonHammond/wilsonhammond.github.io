Core Finding

The control still worked on paper. That was the problem.

This lab treats the incident as control erosion, not a single technical mistake. Vendor reviews existed, but review quality declined. Access policies existed, but recertification failed. Alerts existed, but analyst overload reduced detection value. Training existed, but behavior did not change.

Incident Pattern

How the failure unfolded.

Vendor access survived the project

A third party analytics vendor retained privileged Application Programming Interface access after the business need ended.

Evidence became ritual

Supplier reviews were completed for tracking purposes, but expired documentation and weak proof still passed review.

Ownership dissolved

Procurement, Legal, Security, and Compliance each assumed another group owned expiration tracking and escalation.

Human Control Engine

People, process, and pressure shaped the risk.

  • Security review fatigue turned due diligence into a completion exercise.
  • Approval rubber stamping weakened least privilege.
  • Exception drift allowed temporary business decisions to become permanent risk.
  • Alert desensitization reduced the value of detective controls.
  • Ownership ambiguity delayed escalation and public disclosure coordination.
