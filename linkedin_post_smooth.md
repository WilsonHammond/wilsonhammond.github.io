I built Project Violet Ledger, a Governance, Risk, and Compliance portfolio lab focused on third party cyber risk, control erosion, and enterprise recovery after a simulated vendor compromise.

The scenario follows CivicCare Nexus, a fictional healthcare analytics and payment coordination company that suffers a third party breach after a vendor retains privileged Application Programming Interface access beyond business need.

The project is designed to show more than framework familiarity. It connects vendor inventory, access governance, supplier evidence quality, human control failure, detection gaps, cost exposure, and board level remediation strategy into a single case study.

The most important lesson from the lab is that controls can exist and still fail.

A policy can exist.
A review can exist.
A training record can exist.
An approval workflow can exist.

But if people rush reviews, rubber stamp access, ignore noisy alerts, let exceptions drift, or avoid escalation because ownership is unclear, then the control becomes decorative instead of protective.

Artifacts included:
1. Executive summary
2. Vendor inventory
3. Risk register
4. Control mapping
5. Human control effectiveness assessment
6. Cost exposure model
7. Remediation roadmap
8. GitHub Pages portfolio page

This project strengthened the way I think about Governance, Risk, and Compliance work. The real question is not simply whether a control exists. The stronger question is whether that control still works under pressure.
